# Skilled Immigrants, Firms, and the Global Geography of Innovation

**Author:** Britta Glennon (Wharton, UPenn; NBER)
**Journal:** *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, Vol. 38, No. 1, Winter 2024, pp. 3--26
**DOI:** 10.1257/jep.38.1.3

---

## 1. Research Question
How do skilled immigrants and skilled immigration policy affect firm decision-making, firm outcomes, and the global geography of innovation? What are the comparative merits of supply-driven (points-based) vs. demand-driven (employer-based) immigration systems?

## 2. Motivation / Puzzle
- Highly skilled workers are crucial but scarce inputs into firms' productive and innovative processes; the talent pool is global (59% of AI PhD hires by US firms are immigrants).
- Countries compete to attract top talent but use very different selection systems.
- In the US demand-driven system, firms play the central role: they select, sponsor, and retain skilled immigrants. Yet demand far exceeds supply -- only 23% of international master's students (8% of bachelor's) transition to the US workforce due to visa constraints (Beine, Peri, and Raux 2023).
- Green card wait times for Indian citizens exceed 100 years, creating massive frictions.
- The paper asks: what happens at the firm level and to the global distribution of innovation when access to skilled immigrants is constrained?

## 3. Data
- **H-1B Employer Data Hub** (USCIS): firm-level data on H-1B petition approvals, industry, geography.
- Linkages to **Compustat** (Mayda et al. 2020) and **IRS data** (Doran, Gelber, and Isen 2022) for firm characteristics.
- Cross-country policy comparisons drawing on Papademetriou and Hooper (2019), Aydemir (2020), Boucher and Gest (2018).
- Patent data and venture capital datasets referenced throughout the literature review.

*Note: This is a JEP review/survey article, not an empirical paper with its own novel dataset.*

## 4. Methodology / Identification Strategy
Survey/review article synthesizing the literature. Key identification strategies in cited papers include:
- **H-1B visa lottery** as a natural experiment for causal identification (Doran, Gelber, and Isen 2022; Brinatti et al. 2023).
- **Policy changes** as natural experiments: 2004 H-1B cap reduction (Ghosh, Mayda, and Ortega 2014); Swiss labor reform allowing more skilled workers (Beerli et al. 2021); 2008 OPT STEM extension (Beine, Peri, and Raux 2023).
- Individual-level patent data linking immigrants to innovation outcomes (Hunt 2011; Hunt and Gauthier-Loiselle 2010).

## 5. Key Findings

### Policy Environment (pp. 3--12)
- **Two main systems:** Supply-driven (points-based: Canada, Australia, NZ) vs. demand-driven (employer-based: US, some European countries, Japan, Korea). See **Table 1** (p. 6).
- Points-based systems are transparent and flexible but risk "brain waste" (immigrants unable to find skill-appropriate work). Demand-driven systems ensure economic integration but enable worker exploitation since visas are tied to employers.
- Most points-based countries have recently added employer-selected elements.
- **US visa types** (**Table 2**, p. 9): H-1B (206,002 issued FY2022, cap 85,000 subject to lottery with 42% selection rate); L visa (72,448 for intracompany transfers); O-1 (19,102 for extraordinary ability); TN (2,376 for NAFTA citizens).
- H-1B lottery needed every year since 2004 (cap reduced from 195,000). Since 2014, petitions typically 2--3x the cap.
- OPT program: allows F-1 students 12 months of work (extended to 36 months for STEM in 2016); the 2008 extension raised STEM master's transition rates by 6.5 pp.
- Green card cap: 140,000/year employment-based (14% of all green cards), with 7% per-country limit creating enormous backlogs for India and China.
- **Industry concentration** (**Figure 1**, p. 12): 45.6% of H-1B petitions in Professional, Scientific, and Technical Services; Manufacturing distant second at 11.2%. Top 5 firms (Tata, Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Infosys) = ~10% of all approvals.
- H-1B employers: 37x more R&D spending, 5.7x more employees than non-H-1B firms (Mayda et al. 2020).

### How Skilled Immigration Affects Firm Outcomes (pp. 12--16)
- **Within borders:** Strong positive effects of skilled immigrants on firm innovation, productivity, and performance (Beerli et al. 2021; Brinatti et al. 2023; Ghosh, Mayda, and Ortega 2014), though Doran, Gelber, and Isen (2022) find zero effect on patenting.
- **Three channels for positive effects:**
  1. *Filling labor shortages:* more skilled immigrants $\rightarrow$ optimal human capital stock $\rightarrow$ higher innovation/productivity (works even if immigrants = substitutes for natives).
  2. *Knowledge diversity:* immigrants bring different knowledge from their home countries' technology specializations; novel recombination of knowledge drives innovation (Bahar, Choudhury, and Rapoport 2020; Choudhury and Kim 2019).
  3. *Positive selection:* immigrants drawn from the right tail of talent distribution due to migration frictions (Borjas 1987); comparative advantage specialization (Peri and Sparber 2009).
- **Divergent results explained by firm heterogeneity:** Brinatti et al. (2023) vs. Doran, Gelber, and Isen (2022) differ because the DGI sample (last-day filers in 2005--06) captures firms where immigrants are less critical, while Brinatti et al. use the full 2007 applicant pool. Positive innovation/productivity benefits exist mainly for firms heavily reliant on skilled immigrants.
- **Start-ups:** Immigrants are more likely than natives to start firms of all sizes (Azoulay et al. 2022). Start-ups that hire H-1B workers are more likely to have successful exits (IPO/acquisition) and receive additional VC funding (Dimmock, Huang, and Weisbenner 2022; Chen, Hshieh, and Zhang 2021). Hiring skilled immigrants also improves access to international VC via home-country professional networks (Li 2020).
- **Across borders:** Strong positive relationship between immigrant diasporas and trade, FDI, and investment linkages with countries of origin (Burchardi, Chaney, and Hassan 2019; Balachandran and Hernandez 2021). Three channels: (1) reducing information frictions (market knowledge, business norms); (2) "nostalgia" demand for home-country goods; (3) social capital / contract enforcement where courts are weak.
- **Multinational firm innovation:** US-based ethnic inventor employees helped MNCs form new affiliates abroad and increase innovation in countries related to that ethnicity (Foley and Kerr 2013). MNCs have more cross-border team patents when they have more US-based ethnic researchers (Kerr and Kerr 2018).

### Margins of Adjustment When Immigration Is a Constraint (pp. 16--18)

**Within-US adjustments:**
1. *Production technology change:* Firms may substitute capital for restricted labor. Low-skill evidence: bracero exclusion induced capital-intensive techniques (Clemens, Lewis, and Postel 2018). Capital-skill complementarity literature shows lower-skill immigration leads to less capital-intensive production (Lewis 2011; Peri 2012). By implication, more-skilled labor could lead to more capital-intensive production. AI technologies could also substitute for some high-skilled labor.
2. *Scope / outsourcing:* H-1B cap led to significant rise in outsourcing to third-party firms that specialize in hiring immigrant workers (Sharma and Sparber 2020). Firms also partner with cap-exempt nonprofits (universities, research institutions) to access uncapped H-1B pools -- e.g., Open Avenues Foundation exploits this arbitrage. Firms can create nonprofit research entities (e.g., Microsoft Research) to hire outside the cap.

**Cross-border adjustments:**
- US MNCs facing H-1B restrictions increased foreign affiliate employment, especially in R&D, at both the intensive margin (more employees at existing affiliates) and extensive margin (entering new countries) (Glennon 2023).
- Average MNC created **0.42 foreign affiliate jobs** per unfilled H-1B position; the most internationalized MNCs created **0.93** per unfilled position (not counting new affiliates).
- Some non-multinational firms respond by relocating entirely: US immigrants shifted start-up founding to Canada after Canada's Start-Up Visa Program (Lee and Glennon 2023).

### How Skilled Migrants Shape Cross-Border Innovation (pp. 18--21)
- Hiring skilled immigrant inventors facilitates the disintegration of innovative activity (R&D, patents) across borders. This does *not* come at the cost of the home country -- it is **positive-sum** for home and host countries.
- Restrictive immigration policy causes **reallocation** of global innovative activity. Reforms discouraging inventor migration reduce domestic and global collaborative patenting by MNC subsidiaries and shift innovation toward emerging markets (Bahar et al. 2022).
- **Location matters for innovation quality:** International Math Olympiad participants who go to the US are far more productive; "removing constraints on immigration could increase the global scientific output of future cohorts by up to 50 percent" (Agarwal et al. 2023). Foreign-born US-educated scientists forced to return to low-GDP countries have much lower research output (Kahn and MacGarvie 2016).
- **Brain drain tradeoff:** The lasting economic connections between immigrants and origin countries (emphasized throughout) suggest large potential benefits to sending countries as well (Gibson and McKenzie 2011).

## 6. Mechanisms / Model
This is a survey article, not a theoretical paper. However, the paper organizes findings around an implicit firm-level decision framework with the following mechanisms:

1. **Skilled immigrants as a productive input:** Immigrants fill labor shortages, bring knowledge diversity through novel recombination of home-country technology specializations, and are positively selected from the right tail of the talent distribution.
2. **Diaspora channels for cross-border effects:** Information friction reduction (market knowledge, business norms), nostalgia demand, and social capital / informal contract enforcement.
3. **Firm adjustment margins under immigration constraints:**
   - *Technology substitution:* Change production technology (capital-for-labor substitution).
   - *Boundary of the firm:* Outsource to third-party hiring firms; partner with cap-exempt nonprofits.
   - *Geographic adjustment:* Expand foreign affiliate employment (intensive + extensive margins); relocate entirely.
4. **Innovation geography mechanism:** Immigrant inventors facilitate cross-border R&D disintegration within MNCs $\rightarrow$ innovation spillovers are geographically localized $\rightarrow$ shifting where immigrants work shifts where spillovers occur $\rightarrow$ affects national innovation ecosystems.

The paper argues for richer immigration models embedding firm choices and dynamics, citing Mehra and Shen (2022), Waugh (2018), Olney (2012), Ottaviano, Peri, and Wright (2013), and Morales (2019) as promising starts.

## 7. Policy Implications
- **Demand-side perspective on immigration:** Viewing skilled immigrants only as labor supply substitutes is "fundamentally mistaken." They are job creators (disproportionate firm founders), productivity enhancers, and drivers of innovation and technological progress.
- **Restrictions have unintended consequences:** Limiting skilled immigration does not keep jobs at home; it leads to more offshoring of R&D and employment to foreign affiliates, reducing domestic competitiveness.
- **US competitiveness at stake:** The US demand-driven system creates massive bottlenecks (100+ year green card waits for Indians, lottery randomness). Firms in countries with restrictive policies face a competitive disadvantage, especially non-MNCs that cannot offshore.
- **Global science suffers:** Restricting where top talent can work reduces the overall quality and quantity of global scientific output -- estimated up to 50% increase in global scientific output if immigration constraints were removed (Agarwal et al. 2023).
- **Brain drain is mitigated:** Lasting immigrant--origin-country economic ties (trade, FDI, knowledge transfer, VC) suggest large benefits flow back to sending countries.
- **Need for firm heterogeneity in policy models:** Ignoring firm-level variation in immigrant share leads to underestimation of welfare gains to native workers from immigration by 11% (Brinatti and Morales 2021). US MNCs = one-third of H-1B applications and 80% of US firm R&D.

## 8. Limitations & Open Questions
- **Limited evidence on high-skill technology substitution:** Whether firms respond to skilled immigration restrictions with heavier reliance on AI or capital-intensive production remains unstudied.
- **Firm heterogeneity underexplored:** Which types of firms use which adjustment margins? How prevalent is each response? What interdependencies arise between them?
- **Multinational firms understudied:** Despite being the largest hirers of skilled immigrants (one-third of H-1B applications), MNCs' role is underexplored in immigration models.
- **Nonprofit vs. for-profit firm behavior:** We know "very little" about differences in behavior and outcomes between nonprofit and for-profit firms regarding immigration.
- **Non-MNC responses poorly understood:** How much do non-multinational firms respond to restrictions by becoming MNCs (opening foreign affiliates) vs. relocating entirely?
- **Cap-exempt arbitrage unmeasured:** No empirical evidence measures the prevalence of firms partnering with cap-exempt nonprofits to circumvent H-1B caps.
- **Cross-border evaluation needed:** Immigration research should not be constrained within borders -- a change in one country's policy has implications for innovation, investment, and employment globally.
- **General equilibrium models needed:** More work embedding immigration and offshoring as interconnected decisions in GE models, incorporating MNC activity into welfare analysis.
